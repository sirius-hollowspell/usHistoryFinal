Martha Dandridge Custis Washington

Martha Dandridge Custis Washington

The original first lady, but the title was not coined until after her death. Martha did not enjoy her time in the White House, and preferred living a private life.

Abigail Smith Adams

Abigail Smith Adams

Abigail was the first woman to serve as Second Lady of United States and the second woman to serve as First Lady. She was also the mother of the sixth President, John Quincy Adams.

Martha Wayles Skelton Jefferson

Martha Wayles Skelton Jefferson

Martha passed away 19 years before her husband because president.

Dolley Payne Todd Madison

Dolley Payne Todd Madison

Dolly’s iconic style and social presence boosted her husband’s popularity as President. She remains one of the most well known and beloved ladies of the White House.

Elizabeth Kortright Monroe

Elizabeth Kortright Monroe

Elizabeth was an accomplished hostess but through much of the administration, she was in poor health which curtailed her activities.

Louisa Catherine Johnson Adams

Louisa Catherine Johnson Adams

Louisa was the first of the First Ladies to be born outside of the United States. She came to the US from London, after marrying John Quincy Adams.

Rachel Donelson Jackson

Rachel Donelson Jackson

Rachel died before Andrew Jackson’s inauguration as President, and duties of First Lady fell to her niece, Emily Donelson.

Hannah Hoes Van Buren

Hannah Hoes Van Buren

Hannah died of tuberculosis before Martin Van Buren was elected, leaving him one of the few Presidents to remain unmarried in office.

Anna Tuthill Symmes Harrison

Anna Tuthill Symmes Harrison

Anna was First Lady during her husband’s one-month term in 1841, holding the title for the shortest length of time. She was the first First Lady to be widowed while holding the title.

Letitia Christian Tyler

Letitia Christian Tyler

Letita was the youngest First Lady to pass away and one of only three to have passed away in the White House.

Julia Gardiner Tyler

Julia Gardiner Tyler

Julia was the second wife of John Tyler. She became First Lady from 1844 to 1845 after their secret engagement and wedding.

Sarah Childress Polk

Sarah Childress Polk

Sarah attended one of the very few institutions of higher learning available to women in the early 19th century. While she couldn’t hold a political career of her own, she privately helped her husband with speeches.

Margaret Mackall Smith Taylor

Margaret Mackall Smith Taylor

Due to ill health, Margaret left much of the official hostess duties to her daughter Betty.

Abigail Powers Fillmore

Abigail Powers Fillmore

Abigail met husband President Millard Fillmore when he was her student, and as a teacher she had been the first First Lady to have held a job after marriage.

Jane Means Appleton Pierce

Jane Means Appleton Pierce

While on the journey to Washington D.C., the Pierce’s train derailed and killed their son. Franklin Pierce’s inauguration took place without a ball and without the First Lady.

Harriet Lane

Harriet Lane

Unique among First Ladies, Harriet acted as hostess for the only President who never married: James Buchanan, her favorite uncle and her guardian after she was orphaned at the age of eleven.

Mary Todd Lincoln

Mary Todd Lincoln

Her position fulfilled her high social ambitions, but Mary’s years in the White House mingled misery with triumph. While the Civil War dragged on, Southerners scorned her as a traitor to her birth, and citizens loyal to the Union suspected her of treason. When she entertained, critics accused her of unpatriotic extravagance. She was shattered by Lincoln’s assassination and spent her remaining 17 years with nothing but sorrow.

Eliza McCardle Johnson

Eliza McCardle Johnson

Eliza was a competent, unpretentious, and gracious hostess even during Andrew Johnson’s impeachment crisis.

Julia Dent Grant

Julia Dent Grant

After many years of hardship and stress due to Ulysses Grant’s military career, Julie rejoiced in his fame as a victorious general, and she entered the White House in 1869 to begin “the happiest period” of her life. With Cabinet wives as her allies, she entertained extensively and lavishly.

Lucy Ware Webb Hayes

Lucy Ware Webb Hayes

Lucy entered the White House with confidence gained from her long and happy married life, her knowledge of political circles, her intelligence and culture, and her cheerful spirit. She enjoyed informal parties, and spared no effort to make official entertaining attractive.

Lucretia Rudolph Garfield

Lucretia Rudolph Garfield

Though Lucretia was not particularly interested in a First Lady’s social duties, she was deeply conscientious and her genuine hospitality made her dinners and twice-weekly receptions enjoyable. She served as First Lady until her husband’s assassination.

Ellen Lewis Herndon Arthur

Ellen Lewis Herndon Arthur

Ellen tragically died of pneumonia before Chester Arthur took office.He did not give anyone First Lady duties, but asked his sister to assume some social duties.

Frances Folsom Cleveland

Frances Folsom Cleveland

Frances became the youngest First Lady at age 21; married to Grover Cleveland she was the 23rd and 25th First Lady of the United States.

Caroline Lavinia Scott Harrison

Caroline Lavinia Scott Harrison

Caroline helped found the National Society of the Daughters of the American Revolution (DAR) serving as its first President General. She established the collection of china associated with White House history.

Ida Saxton McKinley

Ida Saxton McKinley

Ida was ill for much of her time as First Lady, but did her best to maintain all social responsibilities. After President McKinley’s assassination, she returned to Ohio.

Edith Kermit Carow Roosevelt

Edith Kermit Carow Roosevelt

Edith knew Theodore Roosevelt from childhood, and was his 2nd wife. She moved to the White House after President McKinley’s assassination.

Helen Herron Taft

Helen Herron Taft

As First Lady, Helen took an interest in politics but concentrated on giving the administration a particular social brilliance. Only two months after the inauguration she suffered a severe stroke, but she was able to return to her role within a year.

Ellen Axson Wilson

Ellen Axson Wilson

Ellen always played a public role with dignity and grace but never learned to enjoy it. She died 1 year into the presidency from Bright’s disease.

Edith Bolling Galt Wilson

Edith Bolling Galt Wilson

Edith was known as “Secret President,” “first woman to run the government” — so legend has labeled a First Lady whose role gained unusual significance when her husband suffered prolonged and disabling illness.

Florence Kling Harding

Florence Kling Harding

When Florence moved into the White House, she opened mansion and grounds to the public again–both had been closed through President Wilson’s illness. She herself suffered from a chronic kidney ailment, but she threw herself into the job of First Lady with energy and willpower.

Grace Anna Goodhue Coolidge

Grace Anna Goodhue Coolidge

Grace was an exceptionally popular White House hostess, she was voted one of America’s 12 greatest living women in 1931.

Lou Henry Hoover

Lou Henry Hoover

Lou was an avid Chinese linguist and geology scholar, she was also the first First Lady to make regular nationwide radio broadcasts. She was an active participant in the Girl Scout movement, including service as its president.

Anna Eleanor Roosevelt

Anna Eleanor Roosevelt

Anna was the longest-serving First Lady throughout her husband President Franklin D. Roosevelt’s four terms in office. In addition to entertaining, she broke precedent to hold press conferences, travel to all parts of the country, give lectures and radio broadcasts, and express her opinions candidly in a daily syndicated newspaper column, “My Day.”

Elizabeth Virginia Wallace Truman

Elizabeth Virginia Wallace Truman

In the White House, its lack of privacy was distasteful to Elizabeth. Though she conscientiously fulfilled the social obligations of her position, she did only what was necessary.

Mamie Geneva Doud Eisenhower

Mamie Geneva Doud Eisenhower

Diplomacy–and air travel–in the postwar world brought changes in their official hospitality. The Eisenhowers entertained an unprecedented number of heads of state and leaders of foreign governments, and Mamie’s evident enjoyment of her role endeared her to her guests and to the public.

Jacqueline Lee Bouvier Kennedy

Jacqueline Lee Bouvier Kennedy

To the role of First Lady, Jacqueline brought beauty, intelligence, and cultivated taste. She devoted much time and study to making the White House a museum of American history and decorative arts as well as a family residence of elegance and charm. Her gallant courage during the tragedy of her husband’s assassination won her the admiration of the world.

Claudia Alta Taylor “Lady Bird” Johnson

Claudia Alta Taylor “Lady Bird” Johnson

Moving to the White House after Kennedy’s murder, Lady Bird did her best to ease a painful transition. She created a First Lady’s Committee for a More Beautiful Capital, then expanded her program to include the entire nation. She took a highly active part in her husband’s war-on-poverty program, especially the Head Start project for preschool children.

Patricia Ryan Nixon

Patricia Ryan Nixon

Pat used her position as First Lady to encourage volunteer service–“the spirit of people helping people.” She took quiet pride in adding 600 paintings and antiques to the White House Collection.

Elizabeth Anne Bloomer Ford

Elizabeth Anne Bloomer Ford

Betty was noted for raising breast cancer awareness and being a passionate supporter of the Equal Rights Amendment.

Rosalynn Smith Carter

Rosalynn Smith Carter

Nancy is remembered for her passionate advocacy for decreasing drug and alcohol abuse. Even after leaving the White House, she continued her campaign to teach children to “just say no” to drugs.

Nancy Davis Reagan

Nancy Davis Reagan

Nancy is remembered for her passionate advocacy for decreasing drug and alcohol abuse. Even after leaving the White House, she continued her campaign to teach children to “just say no” to drugs.

Barbara Pierce Bush

Barbara Pierce

As First Lady, Barbara called working for a more literate America the “most important issue we have.” Involved with many organizations devoted to this cause, she became Honorary Chairman of the Barbara Bush Foundation for Family Literacy.

Hillary Rodham Clinton

Hillary Rodham Clinton

As the nation’s First Lady, Hillary continued to balance public service with private life. Her active role began in 1993 when the President asked her to chair the Task Force on National Health Care Reform. She continued to be a leading advocate for expanding health insurance coverage, ensuring children are properly immunized, and raising public awareness of health issues.

Laura Welch Bush

Laura Welch Bush

A former teacher and librarian, she has dedicated herself to advancing education and promoting the well-being of women and families worldwide. In support of the “No Child Left Behind Act”, Laura launched “Ready to Read, Ready to Learn,” an education initiative that promoted best practices in early childhood education and raised awareness of innovative teacher training programs.

Michelle Obama

Michelle Obama

Michelle is the first African-American First Lady of the United States. During her 8 years as First Lady, she initiated Let’s Move! a program aiming to end childhood obesity within a generation during the first term. During the second term she spearheaded the Reach Higher Initiative to help students understand job opportunities and the education and skills they need for those jobs.

Melania Trump

Melania Trump

Melania announced BE BEST, an awareness campaign that strives to promote a world for children based on healthy living, kindness, and respect.

Dr. Jill Biden

Dr. Jill Biden

Jill has championed the causes that have defined her public life: supporting military families, advocating for increased educational opportunities, and working to end cancer as we know it. She advanced the missions of two key White House initiatives, Joining Forces and the Cancer Moonshot. She is the first presidential spouse to maintain an independent career outside of the White House.
